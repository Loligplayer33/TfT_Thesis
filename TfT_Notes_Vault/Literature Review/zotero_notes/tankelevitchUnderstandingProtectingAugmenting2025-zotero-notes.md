> “AI systems are designed and commonly used to automate capabilities and processes such as writing and research, processes through which people think, and therefore learn, build skills, and grow expertise. The generative nature of these systems means that human workflows are shifting from production to ‘critical integration’ of material [97], i.e., decisions about when and how to use AI, how to frame tasks, and how to assess outputs. Thus, the quality and nature of thinking, such as where and what kind of thinking is applied, all change when knowledge workflows reorient themselves around AI” Red Highlight [Page 2](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/67D5M9ET?page=2&annotation=S83EEGUS)

Critical Point: States that current AI systems automate exactly those activities whose execution help people to learn, build skills and grow expertise. 
The human's role shifted away from those activities towards more detached (from the actual content) orchestration focused activities.

This shift of how people think has observable downstream consequences in Critical Thinking (Section 2.1), Learning (Sections 2.2) and Creativity (Section 2.3) as well as Other domains (Sections 2.4)

The chain they're building is: AI automates the processes through which people think (writing, research, etc.) → because these processes were themselves vehicles for cognition, automating them changes what kind of thinking happens and where → one observable consequence is the workflow shift from production to critical integration → this then manifests across specific cognitive domains (critical thinking, learning, creativity) and contextual factors (workflows, expertise, motivation, well-being).

**Thoughts from AI**
Your argument is good, and it addresses a real gap in how process-oriented support is typically framed. The standard presentation sets up a binary — process-oriented vs. end-to-end — and implies you should pick one. Your point is that from the user's perspective, a system that refuses to help with a complex request is just unhelpful, and unhelpful tools get abandoned. The move you're proposing is more subtle: the system recognizes the request requires higher-order cognition and redirects the form of help rather than withholding it. Summarizing the current state, surfacing open questions, visualizing what's unresolved — that's still genuinely useful output, but it keeps the cognitive work of synthesis and decision-making with the user. That's a stronger design position than "just add friction," because the user doesn't experience it as a refusal. They experience it as a different kind of help. The nudging framing fits here — the system shapes what the user does next without blocking them.

> “Khurana and Chilana [58] identify that a semiautomated (or ‘process-oriented’) approach—automating routine steps, while also guiding users through more complex decisionmaking—is more appropriate when users are less familiar with an application or task, when a task involves complex decision making or creativity (rather than solely repetitive or straightforward steps), or when users have learning goals (rather than solely performance goals; see also [59]).” Yellow Highlight [Page 4](zotero://open-pdf/library/items/67D5M9ET?page=4&annotation=J6UDESA7)

**Thoughts from AI**
Now, does Khurana and Chilana support this? Partially, but not exactly in the way you might want. What they're describing is a selection logic — when should a system use semi-automated support versus full automation? Their answer is conditional: semi-automated is better when users are unfamiliar, when tasks involve complex decision-making, or when learning goals are present. That supports the general principle that not everything should be automated end-to-end. But it doesn't directly support your more specific claim, which is about how the system should respond when it identifies a higher-order task. Khurana and Chilana say "automate routine steps, guide through complex ones" — your argument goes further by specifying what "guiding" should actually look like: not refusing, not asking generic questions, but providing genuinely useful intermediate output that repositions the user as the decision-maker. So Khurana and Chilana give you the when (conditions under which process-oriented support is appropriate), but your contribution is the how (what form that support should take to remain useful without offloading the cognition). You could cite them as establishing the conditions, then position your design logic as filling in the response pattern they leave underspecified.
