# Outcomes

This note develops the argument for what should count as a desirable outcome of a Tool for Thought — what a strategy is *for*, once it has been enacted. Drawing on Rogers et al., outcomes divide into three types: *intermediary outcomes* (tangible artifacts such as notes, diagrams, or drafts that scaffold thinking during use), *cognitive outcomes* (changes in understanding, skill, critical thinking, or metacognitive engagement that persist after use), and *task outcomes* (completion, quality, performance — secondary but not irrelevant). The framework the thesis is building implicitly ends in an outcome: Role → Strategy → Cognitive Function → Outcome. Which outcome type is being protected or augmented, and which is merely supported, is not a settled question in the literature.

Precision note: *cognitive outcomes* (changes in cognition that result from using a TfT — clearer mental models, more accurate self-monitoring, practiced skills) are not the same as *cognitive functions* (the higher-order activities a TfT targets — critical thinking, metacognition, creativity). One is the effect, the other is the target. See [[Glossary]] for cognitive-function definitions; this note tracks outcomes as the downstream measurable changes.

## Paper Contributions

### [[zhang2026tools|zhang2026tools]]
- Three broad types of outcomes are being identified (based on Rogers et al):
	1. Intermediary outcomes: Tangible artifacts that scaffold thinking by externalising (not outsourcing!) abstraction, inference and reflection.
	2. Cognitive outcomes: Changes in understanding, learning or cognitive skills (critical thinking) as well as metacognitive engagement
	3. Task outcomes: The output generated to solve the actual task at hand (this is where the focus is for most GenAI tools - here this is just dimension)
