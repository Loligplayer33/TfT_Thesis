# Adoption and Friction

This note works through the cross-cutting tension between cognitive protection and adoption. Strategies that protect cognition often add friction, and friction triggers user resistance (Ashktorab et al.) and competitive pressure to disable the tool. The note collects what the literature says about why this happens, what sweet-spots have been observed, how customizability and adaptive timing mitigate it, and why the design response cannot be simply "more friction." This tension is not a limitations footnote — it is central to whether any of the strategies in the taxonomy actually survive contact with real users.

## Current Argument

### Working Thoughts

It is extremely important that tools stay useful and provide good user experience. If the tool doesn't get used, it doesn't matter how well it protects or augments cognition.

_Do strategies exist that improve cognitive protection without proportionally degrading usability? Or is the trade-off structural?_ Ashktorab and Kazemitabaar are the two papers most likely to give me traction on that question
