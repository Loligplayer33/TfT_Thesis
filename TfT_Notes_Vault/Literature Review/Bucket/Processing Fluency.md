# AI Answer
Processing fluency is a deeply ingrained heuristic: when information comes easily, your brain interprets that ease as a signal of correctness. This isn't a conscious judgment — it's a pre-reflective metacognitive experience. The cited study makes this vivid: people rate answers as more correct simply because they appeared on screen faster, even though speed has nothing to do with accuracy. Now apply that to GenAI. The system produces a polished, extensive email draft in two seconds. Three things happen simultaneously: <b>The user's confidence in the output goes up</b> — it arrived fast and reads fluently, so it feels right. That's the obvious one.<b>The user's confidence in their own ability to evaluate it goes up</b> — this is the less obvious and more dangerous part. The fluency of the output makes the evaluation feel easy, and that felt ease gets misread as "I'm good at judging this." The passage cites research showing that processing fluency doesn't just affect your confidence in the information — it affects your confidence in your own cognitive performance.<b>The effort the user invests in evaluation goes down</b> — because higher confidence leads to less deliberate processing. The paper cites work showing that when confidence increases, people spend less time thinking and change their minds less often. So the fluency cue inflates confidence, and the inflated confidence reduces the very effort that would catch errors. The chain is: fast, fluent AI output → feels easy to process → metacognitive heuristic interprets ease as accuracy → user's confidence in both the output and their own judgment inflates → user invests less effort in evaluation → errors go undetected. This connects directly to your entanglement argument. The processing fluency doesn't stay contained to "how I feel about the AI's output." It bleeds into "how I feel about my own understanding." After reading the AI's fluent summary, the user's own subsequent thinking about the topic feels more fluent too — because they now have a well-structured frame in their head, courtesy of the AI. They experience that fluency as their own competence. The contamination is happening at the level of metacognitive experience, exactly where it's hardest to detect. For your thesis: this is one of the strongest mechanistic explanations for why cognitive offloading happens even among well-intentioned users. It's not laziness or ignorance — it's that the system generates implicit cues that systematically corrupt the user's self-assessment. Design strategies that counter this need to either disrupt the fluency signal (seamful design, deliberate disfluency) or create alternative evaluation checkpoints that don't rely on the user's felt sense of confidence.
